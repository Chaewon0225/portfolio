Data Mining

I have been applying various educational data mining methods to gain insights about nursing students' learning experiences in the educational escape rooms. Unlike the typical pre-and post-test or ad-hoc survey often used in nursing education literature, such approach provides evidence to inform the optimal design practice for nursing educators who want to design an immersive learning experience without much technological expertise.

Ordered Network Analysis (ONA) (PDF)

ONA handles the fallible tendency of Epistemic Network Analysis, either underrepresenting or even neglecting the temporal nature of collaborative problem-solving by treating collaborations as a set of isolated events (Tan et al., 2022).

I used the analysis to investigate the impact of role allocation on nursing students' collaborative problem-solving in the EER, and found that students tended to second-guess less when they were assigned roles. The study is under review for presentation at AERA 2026.

ONA can be executed using the ENA Web Tool. (https://www.epistemicnetwork.org/)

Sequential Pattern Mining (SPM) (PDF)

I used sequential pattern mining to analyze students' collaborative problem-solving dynamics in the data I collected in 2023. This was when I was applying epistemic network analysis (ENA) to map students' collaborative conceptualizations and realized that ENA does not capture the temporal dynamics.

The paper published in Clinical Simulations in Nursing, also attached on this page in PDF format, leveraged this technique to discover meaningful three-step patterns of facilitators' stop-and-go debriefing in the educational escape rooms designed for first-year nursing students.

You can view the Python code I used for this analysis here.

Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) (PDF)

MDS is a statistical technique for exploratory data analysis and dimension reduction, quantifying similarity judgments from direct ratings or indirect measurements like perceptual confusion ... like perceptual confusion (Hout et al., 2013). I used MDS to investigate the differences in students' gameful experiences after redesigning the escape room following Keller's ARCS theory.

I used SPSS to run the analysis. I can share the tutorial file for this analysis upon request.

This study was presented at AERA 2025 and awarded the Graduate Student Travel Award by SIG-Instructional Technology. I am giving a brief presentation about the study in the photo.
